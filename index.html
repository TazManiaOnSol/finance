The Big Short

Imagine banks are handing out home loans (mortgages) to lots of people, even to those who probably can’t afford to pay them back. These loans are bundled together and sold to investors as "safe" investments, like a box of candy where most pieces are good, but some are spoiled. The investors trust the box because it’s labeled as high-quality, so they keep buying and selling these bundles, making tons of money. But here’s the problem: too many of the loans were given to people who couldn’t afford to pay them back. When they started defaulting (failing to pay), it became clear that those "safe" bundles were full of bad loans. Investors panicked, stopped buying, and the market for these loans collapsed. This caused a chain reaction. Banks lost money because their investments became worthless, and since banks are all connected, the entire financial system started crumbling. People lost homes, businesses couldn’t get loans, and the economy went into a massive recession. It all happened because too much risk was taken without enough checks to make sure it was sustainable.
